Ella Fitzgerald, “The First Lady of Song,”

by Jenna Moon

Tribute to Ella Fitzgerald

Published 3 days ago

Ella Fitzgerald, “The First Lady of Song,” was a legendary American jazz singer of the 20th century, who lived through an era of racial discrimination. Born in 1917, Fitzgerald grew up in a diverse neighborhood in New York, where she enjoyed jazz and dance. However, her life took a difficult turn when her mother passed away, leading her to be sent to a harsh reform school. She escaped alone during the Great Depression, turning to singing and performing on Harlem to make a living. Ella lived to sing and loved to perform. By the end of her career, she had recorded 2,000 songs, earned fourteen Grammy awards.

Album Review

The album, "100 Songs For A Centennial Collection" is a special tribute to Ella Fitzgerald on her 100th birthday, featuring a chronological selection of her best songs at her peak in 2017. This album contains not only her great solo songs but also collaborations with various orchestras and jazz artists during the swing era, such as Chick Webb, Louis Armstrong, The Ink Spots, Count Basie, and Duke Ellington. I can picture her smiling while collaborating with other artists. I adore Ella for her smiles, exceptional talent in interpreting lyrics and melodies, and her scat singing influenced by jazz instrumentals.

A-Tisket, A-Tasket



"People will say something, but you let it go by smiling, I've made more friends than if I was the other way." Her delightful and inviting smile made you want to listen. I love her personality, and I can imagine her smiling while singing “A-Tisket, A-Tasket.” In 1935, Chick Webb, the drummer, and bandleader, met 16-year-old Ella Fitzgerald. Though her sound was unformed, she was able to read music and quickly learn songs. She went on to co-write and record the mega-hit "A-Tisket, A-Tasket," based on the nursery rhyme. The melody and rhythm of "A-Tisket, A-Tasket" are simple and catchy, with clear vocals, and the repetition in the lyrics adds a playful rhythm to the song, even though the lyrics talk about a little girl who is sad after losing her basket. Ella's playful and expressive vocal tone, complemented by lively instruments, defines the song's timbre. She emphasizes lyrics like "market," "drop it," and "peck it" with accented notes, creating a joyful mood. While the song mainly focuses on Ella Fitzgerald's vocals, Chick Webb's band, including drums, brassy trumpets, trombones, saxophones, piano, and bass, contributes to the overall harmony.

I found it interesting to hear the difference in pronunciations of 'either' or 'either' and 'potato' or 'potato' in English when I listened to this song for the first time.
